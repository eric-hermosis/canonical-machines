# Canonical Machines

<a href="https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.18905830">
  <img src="https://zenodo.org/badge/1123506997.svg" alt="DOI">
</a>

This work introduces the theory of canonical machines, a thermodynamic framework for modeling artificial neurons and their ensembles. Neurons are treated as systems in thermal equilibrium, integrating energy and information to generate responses. Activation emerges as an irreversible, entropy-producing process, naturally giving rise to classical activation functions such as $\text{ReLU}$, $\text{SiLU}$, $\text{Swish}$, and $\text{tanh}$. Extending to ensembles, layers of neurons are described as thermodynamic systems whose collective dynamics account for classification, attention, and selective activation. The grand canonical formalism introduces a chemical potential that regulates synaptic suppression and variable participation of units, providing a unified, physically grounded perspective on neural computation and a principled guide for designing and optimizing deep learning architectures.
